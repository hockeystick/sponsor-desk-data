# Lewis & Clark dedicates new Riverbend Environmental Law Center

*Portland, Oregon - September 19, 2025.* The Lewis & Clark
Foundation and Lewis & Clark Law School today dedicated the
Riverbend Environmental Law Center, a new $34 million building on
the law school's southwest Portland campus that houses the
Environmental Law Clinic, a public-interest law library, and
shared workspace for visiting scholars and clinical fellows.

The 38,000-square-foot building replaces a 1972 wing that had been
identified by the college's facilities review as inadequate for
clinic operations since 2017. The capital project was funded by a
$22 million lead gift from a donor who has requested anonymity, $4
million in additional named gifts, and approximately $8 million in
foundation capital reserves authorized by the foundation board in
2021.

"The Riverbend center took eleven years from the first donor
conversation to the ribbon cutting," said Charles Kawamoto,
executive director of the foundation. "The donors who carried this
project were patient. The clinic clients who needed the building
were not, and we owe them better turnaround on the next capital
project."

The Environmental Law Clinic, established in 1989, represents
low-income clients and public-interest plaintiffs in environmental
litigation, with a regional focus on the Columbia River Basin and
the Oregon Coast. The clinic operates with six staff attorneys,
three clinical fellows, and approximately 24 law students per
academic year.

Foundation president Helena Bauer, in remarks at the dedication,
noted that the building's opening coincides with one of the
clinic's most politically visible cases: the pesticide-drift
representation in Marion County Circuit Court that has drawn
critical attention from state legislators and an industry
complaint to the college's accreditor.

"It is appropriate that we are dedicating this building during a
moment of public attention on the clinic's work," Bauer said. "The
clinic does this work whether the year is quiet or loud. The
building is here to support the work, not to advertise it."

The building includes a 220-seat moot court room used by the
clinic for trial preparation and by the law school more broadly
for clinical training, twelve client-meeting rooms with
soundproofing for confidential interviews, and a separate
workspace for the public-interest law library. The library has
been moved from the main law school building and consolidates
materials that had previously been split across three locations.

The dedication ceremony included remarks from Bauer, Kawamoto, law
school dean Marcus Chen, clinic director Naomi Stearns, and three
former clinic clients who agreed to speak publicly about their
cases.
