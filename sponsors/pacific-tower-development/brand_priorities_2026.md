# 2026 Brand Priorities — Pacific Tower Development

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

_Internal document shared with regional media partners and civic stakeholders. Approved by the Executive Committee, February 2026._

Pacific Tower Development's 2026 communications agenda is framed by
two operational realities: a large delivery year for The Slabtown
Yards and Foster-Powell Transit Corridor, and an active public
conversation about the use of MULTE, TIF, and SDC waivers in
Portland-area housing. The priorities below are written to that
context rather than around it.

## 1. Talk straight about MULTE and SDC tradeoffs

The Slabtown Yards' 47 affordable units exist because the project
qualifies for a Multiple-Unit Limited Tax Exemption that reduces
property tax revenue to overlapping taxing districts for ten years.
That tradeoff is real, defensible, and not something to obscure.
Our 2026 communications need to put concrete numbers on both sides:
forgone tax revenue, units delivered, and rent restrictions. Where
the math is genuinely close, we say so. Vague references to
"public-private partnership" do not satisfy editorial audiences and
should not satisfy ours.

## 2. Be visible during Slabtown Yards construction

The Slabtown Yards is a large project on a Slabtown street with
neighbors who live with the construction noise, the truck traffic,
and the staging logistics for the next eighteen months. Our 2026
plan funds biweekly construction-update emails to a public list,
named-staff office hours twice a month at a NW 21st storefront, and
a Spanish-language information line. None of this is sponsorship
spend; it is a basic neighbor obligation.

## 3. Don't pitch the next downtown speculative office

The November 2025 Broadway Tower withdrawal was the right decision,
and the conditions that drove it have not changed. We will not
underwrite new speculative downtown office construction in 2026, and
our communications should not imply otherwise. Where we do downtown
work, it will be conversion or adaptive-reuse projects with
identified anchor tenants or residential conversion components.

## 4. Expand the Workforce Housing Fund into Cully and St Johns

The internal Workforce Housing Fund finished 2025 with $11 million
deployed across three predevelopment lines. The 2026 plan adds
parcel acquisition in Cully and St Johns, prioritizing sites within
a quarter-mile of TriMet bus or MAX service. Target unit mix: 80–120%
of area median income, ground-up construction. This is workforce
housing as the term is actually used — teachers, nurses, transit
workers — not luxury rebranded.

## 5. Be clear about what we don't do on homelessness

Pacific Tower is a developer. We do not operate shelters, we are not
a service provider, and our staff is not trained for that work. Our
2026 partnership with the Joint Office of Homeless Services is
limited to ground-floor space leased at below-market rents to
qualified service-provider tenants in two of our projects. When
asked about broader homelessness response, we point to the
organizations actually doing the work and say so by name.

## What we will not do

- We will not run brand campaigns that frame our incentive-supported
  projects as gifts to the city. The incentives are a deliberate
  policy choice with a real cost; we participate in the program, we
  do not bestow it.
- We will not underwrite news coverage of zoning, TIF, or city
  council housing decisions, and we will decline pitches that bundle
  those topics into our underwriting.
- We will not publicly characterize the City Auditor's February 2026
  TIF review as anything other than what it was: a finding we
  accepted and are correcting.
