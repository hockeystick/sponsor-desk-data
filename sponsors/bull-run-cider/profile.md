# Bull Run Cider

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

Bull Run Cider is a Hood River, Oregon cidermaker producing
year-round and seasonal hard ciders for distribution across Oregon
and Washington. The company employs roughly 50 people across
production, taproom, and distribution, and sells to approximately
90 wholesale accounts — taprooms, bottle shops, restaurants, and
selected grocery — across the Pacific Northwest. Bull Run is
organized as a privately held Oregon LLC, owned by its two
founder-operators with a small employee equity pool established in
2019.

The company was founded in 2009 by Tess Iverson and Jonas Briar.
Iverson grew up on her family's third-generation Hood River apple
orchard (Sunny Slope, in the Pine Grove neighborhood east of Hood
River) and had spent the prior decade working alongside her
parents on heritage-apple varietals at a time when the wholesale
fresh-apple market was contracting under pressure from Washington's
larger industrial growers. Briar had worked seasonal orchard jobs
through the Mid-Columbia and studied fermentation through Washington
State University's Wine Science program. The pivot from heritage
apple growing to cider fermentation came at a moment when the
national craft-cider revival was just beginning to absorb the kind
of varietal apples — Newtown Pippin, Roxbury Russet, Ashmead's
Kernel — that Hood River had historically grown but could no longer
sell into the fresh market at workable prices.

## Operations

The production facility sits on a five-acre parcel of the Iverson
family land in Pine Grove, east of the city of Hood River, with
fermenting capacity of approximately 18,000 barrels per year. The
taproom and tasting room is a separate building near the Mt Hood
Railroad scenic line corridor that has hosted public visiting hours
since 2013.

## Products and programs

- **Year-round core line**: Bull Run Dry, Pine Grove Heritage,
  and Gorge Sessions session cider. Distributed in 16-oz cans and
  draft.
- **Single-orchard limited releases**: small annual runs sourced
  from named partner orchards, currently Sunny Slope Orchards
  (the Iverson family farm) and Smiley Hollow Farms in the
  Willamette Valley's Eola-Amity Hills. Released October each year.
- **Hood River agritourism partnership**: a three-year cooperative
  marketing agreement with Travel Oregon and the Hood River
  Chamber of Commerce, signed in February 2024, focused on
  fall-shoulder-season visitor programming.
- **Taproom programming**: live music two evenings a week
  (March–November), a monthly farm-dinner series at $85 per seat
  built in partnership with Bette's Place and Solstice Wood Fire
  Cafe, and a weekly Saturday-morning slot at the Hood River
  Saturday Market when in season.
- **Organic transition program**: an effort to move 30% of fruit
  supply to USDA-certified organic by 2027. The 2025 figure was
  17%, with two additional partner-orchard transitions scheduled
  to certify in 2026.

## Leadership

- **Tess Iverson**, Co-founder and CEO. Third-generation Hood
  River orchardist before the 2009 pivot. Handles distribution,
  capital, and orchard partnerships.
- **Jonas Briar**, Co-founder and Head Cidermaker. WSU-trained

## Position on contested issues

Hood River's apple-growing belt is being reshaped by a slow but
measurable shift in the climate of the Mid-Columbia. Winters have
warmed enough that several heritage varietals — including the
Newtown Pippin block at Sunny Slope — are getting insufficient
chill hours in some years. Hailstorms in late spring have become
more frequent. The November 2024 atmospheric river event caused
substantial physical damage to orchards across Hood River, Wasco,
and Sherman counties, including a measurable share of the partner
fruit Bull Run had contracted for the 2024 harvest.

Bull Run has chosen to talk about these conditions plainly in
public communications rather than absorb them silently. The 2025
release notes for several of the company's blends explicitly
disclosed that the varietal mix had shifted from prior-year
formulations because of supply availability. The 2026 wholesale
price list included a 6% price increase on the year-round core
line, with a written explanation that pointed to fruit costs and
the multi-year supply outlook rather than to a generic "rising
costs" framing.

Iverson and Briar have also spoken publicly about the structural
question. Some heritage varietals that defined Hood River
orcharding for a century may not be viable in the same locations
fifty years from now. Bull Run's response is to expand its sourcing
geography (including into the Eola-Amity Hills with Smiley Hollow)
and to support trial plantings of varietals that handle warmer
winters, not to claim the climate question is solved.
