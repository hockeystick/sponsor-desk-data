# Portland Museum of Art

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

The Portland Museum of Art is Oregon's largest art museum, founded
in 1892 and located on the South Park Blocks in downtown Portland.
The museum operates as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit with a permanent
collection of approximately 50,000 works, an annual operating
budget of $22.4 million, and an endowment of $76 million as of the
fiscal year ending June 2025. Membership stands at approximately
22,000 households.

The museum's two existing buildings — the 1932 Belluschi Building
and the 1925 Masonic Temple, acquired and renovated in 1992 — face
each other across SW Madison Street and are connected at the
basement level. The planned Rothko Pavilion, scheduled to open in
late 2026, will link the two buildings above grade for the first
time.

## Programs

- **Title I Free Day program**: the museum's signature K–12
  access initiative. Free admission and a curriculum-aligned
  docent tour for every Title I school in the Portland Public
  Schools, David Douglas, Reynolds, Centennial, and Parkrose
  districts. In 2025 the program added a transportation grant of
  $180 per bus-day, capped at 200 buses per year, to address the
  finding that bus costs were the binding constraint for
  participating schools rather than admission.
- **Two free Sundays per month**: in November 2024 the museum
  doubled its free-admission day from one Sunday per month to
  two. 2025 attendance on free Sundays averaged 4,100 visitors,
  approximately 2.6 times paid-Sunday attendance.
- **PMA Regional Commissions**: a biennial commissioning program
  for Pacific Northwest artists. The 2025–27 cohort consists of
  six artists at $45,000 each, with exhibition and a printed
  catalogue produced by the museum's curatorial department.
- **Repatriation work**: a multi-year program of consultation
  and return with the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs and
  other Pacific Northwest tribal cultural-resources offices. The
  museum's stated position is that NAGPRA compliance is the
  floor, not the ceiling. Two specific returns were completed in
  2024–25; a third is under active consultation.
- **Rothko Pavilion expansion**: a 30,000-square-foot capital
  project linking the museum's two buildings, named for Mark
  Rothko, who attended Lincoln High School in Portland. The
  capital campaign has raised $108 million against a $120 million
  target as of February 2026.

## Leadership

- **Dr. Eleanor Tagaq**, Director and CEO since 2022. Previously
  deputy director at the Seattle Asian Art Museum; before that,
  curator of contemporary art at the Anchorage Museum. Tagaq, of
  Iñupiaq heritage, holds a PhD in art history from the
  University of Washington and a BA from Reed College.
- **Marisol Reyes**, Chief Curator since 2020. Previously
  associate curator of painting and sculpture at SFMOMA. Reyes
  authored the catalogue for the museum's 2024 retrospective on
  Lucinda Parker and is leading curatorial planning for the
  Rothko Pavilion's inaugural exhibitions.
- **Kenji Howe**, Director of Education. Twelve-year tenure at
  the museum. Designed the original Title I Free Day program in
  2018 and led the 2025 expansion to add the transportation
  grant.

## Position on contested issues

The museum has acknowledged in writing that portions of its
historical Pacific Northwest Indigenous collection were acquired,
in the period roughly 1890–1960, in conditions that the museum
now describes as coercive — including purchases from non-tribal
intermediaries during the Termination era and acquisitions from
estate sales where tribal provenance had not been adequately
documented. The museum's repatriation work, conducted in
consultation with the cultural-resources offices of the
Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs and other Pacific Northwest
tribes, treats those acquisition histories as facts to be
reckoned with rather than as legal complications to be managed.

In 2024 the museum returned a set of nine Wasco-style baskets
(collected 1923) and a set of ceremonial regalia (acquired 1957
from a private estate) to the Confederated Tribes of Warm
Springs, following a multi-year consultation process. A third
holding is under active consultation as of early 2026; the
museum has not publicly identified the holding at the request of
the consulting tribal cultural-resources office.

The museum's policy, adopted by the board of trustees in March
2024, holds that the federal Native American Graves Protection
and Repatriation Act of 1990 sets a minimum standard, and that
the museum will consult with tribal cultural-resources offices on
all Pacific Northwest Indigenous holdings regardless of whether
NAGPRA strictly applies to a given object. Director Eleanor Tagaq
has stated publicly that the museum does not present this work as
a curatorial accomplishment.

The Rothko Pavilion expansion drew concern during the 2023–24
design review from neighborhood and disability-access advocates
about the originally proposed elevated walkway, which would have
routed all between-building traffic through stairs and a single
elevator. The museum revised the design in mid-2024 to add an
at-grade ADA-compliant entrance directly off SW Park Avenue. The
revision added approximately $4 million to project cost and was
absorbed within the existing capital campaign target.
