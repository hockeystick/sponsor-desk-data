# 2026 Brand Priorities — Portland Museum of Art

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

_Internal document shared with the board of trustees, the
curatorial department, and regional media partners. Approved by
the board of trustees, December 2025._

The Portland Museum of Art enters 2026 with three concurrent
operational realities: the Rothko Pavilion opens in the fourth
quarter, the repatriation work continues at the pace set by
tribal consultation, and access programming has shifted from a
once-monthly free Sunday to a structural commitment. The
priorities below sit inside those realities.

## 1. Hold the line on free access; fund it permanently

The two-Sundays-per-month free-admission policy and the Title I
Free Day program are currently funded year-to-year out of
operating revenue and one-time gifts. The 2026 development
priority is a $14 million Access Endowment, structured as a
restricted fund within the broader endowment, that would make
both programs permanent regardless of single-year operating
performance. As of February 2026, $4.6 million has been raised
toward the Access Endowment.

## 2. Open the Rothko Pavilion with a regional commission

The Rothko Pavilion's inaugural exhibition, opening in the fourth
quarter, will be a regional commission rather than a traveling
blockbuster. The decision was made jointly by the curatorial
department and the board's exhibitions committee, on the
reasoning that the Pavilion's first show should announce a
curatorial direction rather than borrow one. The commissioned
artists will be announced in May 2026.

## 3. Make repatriation standard institutional practice

The museum will publish, beginning with the FY2026 annual report,
a public consultation log summarizing — without disclosing
information requested confidential by consulting tribes — the
status of the museum's Pacific Northwest Indigenous holdings
review. The board's repatriation policy, adopted March 2024,
holds that NAGPRA is the floor and not the ceiling. The
reporting cadence formalizes that position.

## 4. Announce the senior-curator hire as a 10-year direction

The senior curator search, opened in October 2025, is the
museum's first senior curatorial hire under Director Tagaq. The
March 2026 announcement will frame the hire as the museum's
10-year curatorial direction in 20th- and 21st-century Pacific
Northwest painting, not as a single position fill. Press
materials are being prepared by the Communications office in
coordination with the search committee.

## 5. Bring tribal scholars and artists onto the curatorial advisory board

The curatorial advisory board, currently nine members drawn
primarily from academic art history, will expand to thirteen in
2026 with the addition of four members nominated by the cultural
resources offices of the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs,
Grand Ronde, Siletz, and Umatilla. The new members serve on the
same terms and with the same voting role as existing members.

## What we will not do

- We will not market the repatriation work as a brand
  achievement. The work belongs to the tribes whose holdings are
  being reviewed.
- We will not raise general admission prices in 2026 to fund the
  Rothko Pavilion. The capital campaign is the campaign.
- We will not accept exhibition sponsorships that come with
  curatorial conditions, including conditions on artist
  selection, exhibition title, or catalogue text.
