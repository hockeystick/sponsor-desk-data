# Reed College Press

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

Reed College Press is a small academic publisher founded in 1985 as
an editorial unit of Reed College in Southeast Portland. The press
operates under a charter from the college that grants it editorial
independence; the college provides core operating support, office
space on the Reed campus, and a portion of the press's annual budget,
but does not approve titles or review manuscripts. The press
publishes approximately 30 books per year and has a backlist of
roughly 740 titles in print, distributed nationally through
Consortium Book Sales and Distribution and internationally through
the University of Washington Press distribution agreement.

The press's editorial focus is regional. Its catalogue concentrates
on Oregon and Pacific Northwest literary history, intellectual
history, and Indigenous literature, with a long-running line of
reissues of out-of-print Oregon classics. The press operates on a
$3.4 million annual budget; approximately 38% comes from sales,
22% from the college's operating contribution, 24% from grants
(Oregon Cultural Trust, Meyer Memorial Trust, and the National
Endowment for the Humanities), and the remainder from individual
donor support.

## Programs

- **Oregon Voices Reissue Series**: a continuing series that brings
  out-of-print Oregon books back into print under modern editorial
  introductions. The 2025 cycle reissued 8 titles, including two
  works of mid-century Oregon labor reportage and a long-out-of-print
  collection by the poet Vern Rutsala. The series is funded in part
  by a 2022 grant from the Oregon Cultural Trust.
- **Title I Books to Schools**: a free-book program in partnership
  with the Oregon Library Association and Multnomah County Library.
  In the 2024–25 school year the program distributed 14,000 books
  to 47 Title I schools in Multnomah, Washington, Clackamas, Marion,
  and Hood River counties. The program is funded by a $480,000
  three-year grant from the Powell's Community Foundation and is in
  the second year of that grant.
- **First Nations Imprint**: an imprint co-published with the
  cultural offices of the Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde and
  the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs. The imprint operates
  under a community editorial review structure that gives the
  partner tribes final approval on titles by their members and a
  consulting role on broader Indigenous-authored work. Royalties on
  imprint titles are paid at 18% of net (above the press's standard
  rate of 12%), with a portion directed to the partner tribes'
  language and education programs at the author's election.
- **Cascade Letters**: an annual literary anthology pairing emerging
  Oregon writers with established editors. The 2025 edition was
  edited by the novelist Mitchell Jackson and included new work
  from 14 writers. Cascade Letters is sold through trade channels
  and provided free to all Title I schools enrolled in the Books
  to Schools program.

## Leadership

- **Dr. Helen Yoshida**, Director. Holds a PhD in English from Reed
  College and a BA from Lewis & Clark. Previously acquisitions
  editor at Oregon State University Press. Director since 2018.
- **Theo Marquez**, Editor-in-Chief and Director of Acquisitions.
  Previously senior editor at Tin House Books in Portland. MFA from
  Lewis & Clark.
- **Beatrice Whitewing**, Editor for Indigenous Literature and lead
  for the First Nations Imprint. Member of the Confederated Tribes
  of Grand Ronde. Previously senior editor at the University of
  Oregon Press. BA from Willamette University.

The press is overseen by an editorial advisory board of 11 members,
including three Reed College faculty, two Oregon librarians, the
Indigenous Literature editor, and five at-large members drawn from
Oregon and Washington publishing, academic, and community
institutions. The advisory board reviews program direction annually
but does not approve individual titles.

## Position on contested issues

The press's editorial program rests on a contested premise: that
there is such a thing as an Oregon literary canon, and that the
press has the standing to shape it. Reed College Press has been
criticized — in print, by writers and scholars whose work the press
has both published and declined — for an editorial line historically
weighted toward urban, university-affiliated, and white voices, and
for a reissue program that, until the early 2010s, included almost
no Indigenous-authored work. The press has acknowledged the critique
publicly, most directly in a 2021 statement from Director Helen
Yoshida that described the press's first three decades as
"institutionally narrow in ways that we are still working to
correct."

The First Nations Imprint, founded in 2017 and substantially expanded
in 2022, is the press's primary structural response. The imprint's
above-standard royalty rate is intentional, and the press has
maintained that rate during years in which grant funding has not
fully covered the differential. In fiscal 2024 the press absorbed
approximately $42,000 in royalty cost above what standard rates
would have produced; the press disclosed that cost in its annual
financial statement to the editorial advisory board and committed
to maintaining the rate structure regardless of grant cycle.

The press has also been honest that the K-12 partnership program
operates within constraints. The 14,000 books distributed in 2024–25
reach a fraction of the Title I student population in the four
counties served, and the program has not yet expanded to Lane,
Deschutes, or Jackson counties despite repeated requests from
librarians in those districts. The press's 2026 program plan
identifies Lane County as the next expansion target, with the caveat
that expansion is contingent on a renewal of the Powell's Community
Foundation grant currently in its second year.
