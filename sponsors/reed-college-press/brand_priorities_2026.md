# 2026 Brand Priorities — Reed College Press

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

_Internal document shared with regional media partners and civic stakeholders. Approved by the Editorial Advisory Board, January 2026._

Reed College Press is a small publisher with a regional brief. Our
2026 priorities reflect that scale honestly: we publish around 30
books a year, we serve a clearly bounded geography, and our
audience is built one librarian, one bookseller, and one writer
at a time. The priorities below name what we will spend on and
what we will not.

## 1. Carry the Books to Schools program through the second year

The Powell's Community Foundation grant funding Title I Books to
Schools is in year two of three. We will distribute approximately
16,000 books in the 2025–26 school year, up from 14,000 in 2024–25,
and add seven additional schools in Hood River and Clackamas
counties. The program's third-year expansion to Lane County is
contingent on a grant renewal we will pursue in the fall of 2026.

## 2. Honor the spring 2026 Vern Rutsala posthumous publication

The collected late poems of Vern Rutsala, prepared in coordination
with the poet's estate, will publish in April 2026 with an initial
print run of 4,000. The publication is a significant moment for the
Oregon Voices Reissue Series and a longer one for the press as an
institution. Public communications around the release should
foreground the work, the editorial team that prepared it, and the
Oregon poets who contributed afterwords, rather than the press
itself.

## 3. Publish the First Nations Imprint spring list with the partner tribes leading

The First Nations Imprint's spring 2026 list includes three titles,
including a debut novel from a Warm Springs author and a new edition
of a 1978 Grand Ronde oral history collection. Public communications
for these titles will be coordinated with the partner tribes'
cultural offices, and the partner tribes will lead any media
appearances they choose to do. The press's role is logistics and
distribution, not narration.

## 4. Maintain the regional editorial line

The press will continue to publish work by, about, and for Oregon
and the Pacific Northwest. We will not extend the editorial program
to national-trade subjects to chase sales. The press's identity is
regional, the audience knows it is regional, and the catalogue is
strongest when it is honest about that.

## 5. Be useful to regional newsrooms

The press maintains an open-permissions agreement with The Cascade
Tribune and three other regional outlets that allows the outlets to
quote up to 800 words from any in-print title at no charge for use
in cultural and literary coverage. We will renew that agreement in
2026 and add up to two additional regional outlets at editorial
discretion. The agreement does not give the press any review of the
coverage itself.

## What we will not do

- We will not pursue national-trade titles outside the regional
  editorial program, even where individual proposals are commercially
  attractive. The press has tried this twice (in 2008 and 2014); it
  did not work, and the diversion of editorial attention damaged
  both the regional list and the experimental titles.
- We will not bring on celebrity-author titles for promotional
  purposes if the work does not meet the editorial standard the
  Indigenous Literature editor and the Editor-in-Chief apply to
  every other manuscript.
- We will not reduce First Nations Imprint royalty rates to align
  with the press's standard rate. The 18% imprint rate is a
  structural commitment.
