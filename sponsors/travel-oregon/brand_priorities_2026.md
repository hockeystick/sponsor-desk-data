# 2026 Brand Priorities — Travel Oregon

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

_Internal document shared with the Tourism Commission, regional
destination management organizations, and media partners. Approved
by the Tourism Commission, January 2026._

Travel Oregon's 2026 communications agenda is shaped by two facts
the agency has stopped trying to talk past. First, the lodging tax
is collected from every Oregon county, and the marketing benefit
has not historically been distributed in proportion. Second, the
agency operates under public-records law, not under brand
discretion, and the 2024–25 ORS 192 litigation made clear that the
agency's prior records practice was not aligned with what state
law requires. Both facts shape the priorities below.

## 1. Move more dollars east of the Cascades

The 2025–27 biennial budget put a 40% rural-allocation floor in
place — up from the 28% effective rate in the 2023–25 cycle. The
2026 work is to make that floor real at the program level: Rural
Tourism Studio cohort selection, Industry Affairs grants, and
Accessibility Project capital grants will each report rural-share
figures publicly, by region, in the Q3 2026 budget update.

## 2. Make the Oregon Tribal Tourism Initiative permanent

The Initiative was funded as a pilot in 2024 with two-year sunset
language. The 2027–29 budget request, drafted in 2026, will move
the Initiative to a permanent line item with a $4.0 million
biennial floor. The agency's role is administrative; programmatic
direction stays with the nine tribes through the existing MOU
structure.

## 3. Publish marketing-contractor invoices on the open data portal

Beginning April 2026, Travel Oregon will post all
marketing-contractor invoices over $10,000 on the agency's open
data portal within 30 days of payment, with vendor names
unredacted. Personal-identifying information for contractor
employees stays redacted under the standard ORS 192.345 exemption.
This replaces the prior practice of releasing the same data only
on records request and supersedes the redaction protocol that drew
the 2024 litigation.

## 4. Treat accessibility as a baseline

The Accessibility Project's lodging-grant work continues. The 2026
addition: every Travel Oregon-funded marketing asset — including
photography, video, and web content produced by agency contractors
— must pass screen-reader and keyboard-navigation testing before
publication. Contracts signed after March 1, 2026, will include
this requirement in the scope of work.

## 5. Build coastal-overflow programming with rural counties

The Oregon Coast's lodging supply is fixed; demand isn't. The
2026 program of work, in coordination with the Eastern Oregon and
Southern Oregon regional bodies, builds itineraries that move
overflow coastal demand inland — the Painted Hills, the Wallowas,
the Rogue Valley wineries, the Klamath Basin. Initial budget
allocation: $1.1 million.

## What we will not do

- We will not return to the 2023-era international-marketing
  contractor model that produced the records dispute.
- We will not run brand campaigns that paper over the rural
  funding-equity question. The data is on the open data portal,
  and our communications will reflect what's in the data.
- We will not extend underwriting agreements to outlets that
  cannot maintain editorial firewalls on tourism-policy coverage.
