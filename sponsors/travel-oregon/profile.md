# Travel Oregon

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

Travel Oregon is the trade name of the Oregon Tourism Commission, a
semi-independent state agency established in 2003 under HB 2267. The
agency is headquartered in Salem with a satellite office in
Portland's Old Town, and operates with a biennial budget of
approximately $60 million — roughly $30 million per fiscal year —
funded primarily by a 1.5% statewide transient lodging tax (TLT). A
nine-member Tourism Commission, appointed by the Governor and
confirmed by the Senate, governs the agency.

The agency's statutory mandate has two parts: marketing Oregon as a
travel destination, and supporting rural-economic-development work
in the seven travel regions — Eastern Oregon, Central Oregon, the
Oregon Coast, the Columbia River Gorge, the Mt. Hood/Willamette
Valley, the Southern Willamette Valley, and Southern Oregon. Travel
Oregon's programmatic work reaches every Oregon county.

## Programs

- **Rural Tourism Studio**: a cohort-based community-tourism
  development program that admits roughly twelve communities per
  biennium. The 2025–27 cohort includes Burns, Joseph, Lakeview,
  Tillamook, Vale, and seven additional rural communities. Total
  biennial allocation: $5.2 million.
- **Oregon Tribal Tourism Initiative**: launched in 2024 under
  formal MOUs with the nine federally recognized Oregon tribes.
  First-cycle programmatic grants ($1.8 million) were awarded in
  September 2025 to six tribally led tourism projects, including
  cultural-tourism programming at the Confederated Tribes of Grand
  Ronde and the Confederated Tribes of Siletz Indians.
- **Travel Oregon Accessibility Project**: an accessibility-audit
  and capital-grant program for rural lodging operators. Funded at
  $2.4 million for the 2025–27 biennium, administered in
  partnership with Disability Rights Oregon.
- **Oregon Coast Visitor Recovery Fund**: a $4.6 million biennial
  allocation supporting smaller coastal-county operators in
  Tillamook, Lincoln, Curry, and Coos counties — an explicit
  response to the post-2020 recovery gap between coastal lodging
  and coastal small-operator revenue.
- **Industry Affairs grants**: a competitive small-grant pool
  (~$3.8 million per biennium) directed to destination management
  organizations and chambers of commerce, with a rural-county
  set-aside that the agency raised from 30% to 40% in the 2025–27
  cycle.

## Position on contested issues

The agency's most persistent operational challenge is the
distribution of TLT-funded marketing dollars across Oregon's
geography. Eastern Oregon legislators — most consistently the
delegations from Harney, Lake, Wheeler, Grant, Malheur, and Baker
counties — have raised in successive legislative sessions that the
historical share of marketing spend routed through coastal and
Portland-metro promotion has run as high as 62%. Travel Oregon
acknowledged the imbalance in 2024 testimony to the House Committee
on Economic Development and committed to a 40% rural-allocation
floor in the 2025–27 biennial budget. CEO Aaron Beckwith, in
February 2025 testimony, framed the shift as overdue rather than
generous: "The lodging tax is collected statewide. The marketing
benefit has not been distributed statewide. We're correcting that."

Travel Oregon was also the subject of a 2024 public-records
lawsuit. A reporter, Marina Pell, then with the Salem Reporter,
filed a request in late 2023 for unredacted contractor invoices
tied to a 2023 international-marketing campaign. The agency
released documents with substantial redactions; Pell sued under
ORS 192. In May 2025 the agency settled, released the records in
substantially less redacted form, paid $48,000 in plaintiff's
attorneys' fees, and adopted a revised records-request workflow
that includes a presumption against vendor-name redaction. Beckwith
publicly stated that the original redactions had been overbroad.

Travel Oregon maintains an editorial-firewall policy on any
sponsored journalism it underwrites, and has on three occasions in
2024–2025 declined to extend underwriting to outlets that could
not maintain the firewall on tourism-policy coverage.
