# Pacific Northwest Health

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

Pacific Northwest Health is an integrated nonprofit health system
operating 12 hospitals across Oregon and Southwest Washington, with
approximately 22,000 employees and an additional 4,800 affiliated
physicians and advanced-practice clinicians. The system traces its
founding to 1875, when the Sisters of the Holy Names opened a
six-bed infirmary in what is now Old Town Portland. The organization
operated under Catholic sponsorship until a 2003 reorganization that
transferred sponsorship to a secular nonprofit board while
preserving the system's charitable mission and the original
mission-care commitments to uninsured patients.

The system's anchor facilities are Pacific Northwest Medical Center
in Northwest Portland (a 412-bed academic-affiliated hospital that
partners with OHSU on residency training) and Pacific Northwest
Beaverton (a 286-bed community hospital). Smaller facilities serve
Hood River, Astoria, Newberg, Tualatin, Vancouver WA, Salem, and
two locations in East Multnomah County. The system reported
operating revenue of $5.8 billion in fiscal 2025 and an operating
margin of 1.4%, below its five-year average.

## Programs

- **Cascade Behavioral Health Center, Lents**: a $94 million
  behavioral-health build-out at SE 92nd and Foster, opened in
  phased fashion beginning September 2025. Phase 1 brought 32 crisis
  stabilization beds online; Phase 2, scheduled for late 2026, adds
  46 additional beds and an outpatient clinic. The project is
  co-funded with Multnomah County and the Oregon Health Authority
  and was scoped in part to absorb demand created by the 2024
  recriminalization of personal-use drug possession that followed
  the partial repeal of Measure 110.
- **Rural Connect Telehealth**: a 24/7 specialist consult network
  for rural partner hospitals, currently active at Hood River
  Memorial and Columbia Memorial in Astoria. The program handled
  approximately 38,000 consults in 2025, most heavily in cardiology,
  neurology, and psychiatric crisis assessment.
- **Maternal Health Initiative**: a $22 million five-year program
  (2024–2029) targeting maternal mortality and severe maternal
  morbidity, with a particular focus on Black and Indigenous
  patients, who experience outcomes substantially worse than the
  system average. The initiative funds a home-visit doula program,
  a fourth-trimester clinic, and an internal case-review process
  that publishes anonymized outcome data annually.
- **Emergency Department Capacity Plan**: a 2024 commitment to add
  44 inpatient beds across three Portland-area emergency
  departments by year-end 2026, in response to documented boarding
  of admitted patients in ED hallways during the post-pandemic
  capacity crunch. As of February 2026 the system has delivered 28
  of the 44 beds.
- **Charity Care and Financial Assistance Program**: revised in 2025
  to apply automatic eligibility screening for patients earning
  under 300% of federal poverty guidelines, replacing the prior
  application-based model.

## Position on contested issues

Pacific Northwest Health and the Oregon Nurses Association have been
in active contract negotiations across most of 2025. A five-day
strike in October 2024, which preceded the current bargaining cycle,
remains the longest nursing strike in the system's history. CEO
Aniko Reyes acknowledged in a January 2026 statement to staff that
several of the unresolved issues — particularly nurse-to-patient
ratios on medical-surgical floors and the structure of charge-nurse
differentials — reflect real operational gaps that the system has
not yet closed. Negotiations are continuing without a public
timeline.

In late 2023 a Cascade Tribune investigation found that Pacific
Northwest Health had pursued aggressive collections against patients
who were eligible for, but had not been screened for, charity care
under the system's existing financial-assistance policy. The system
did not contest the reporting. Pacific Northwest Health refunded
$11.4 million in collections to approximately 6,200 patients in
2024 and rebuilt its charity-care eligibility process to apply
screening automatically at the point of registration. The system
has agreed to publish annual third-party audit data on charity-care
denials beginning with fiscal year 2026.

The system's rural emergency departments — particularly the 18-bed
ED in Astoria and the 14-bed ED in Hood River — operate with
staffing models that the system itself has described as fragile.
Pacific Northwest Health has not committed to expanding inpatient
capacity at either facility and has been transparent that doing so
would require operating subsidies the system is not currently
prepared to extend. The Rural Connect telehealth program is the
system's stated answer to the gap; it is not a complete one.
