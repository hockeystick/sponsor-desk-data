# 2026 Brand Priorities — Pacific Northwest Health

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

_Internal document shared with regional media partners and civic stakeholders. Approved by the System Communications Committee, February 2026._

Pacific Northwest Health enters 2026 with three operational realities
shaping its public communications: an unresolved nursing contract,
a behavioral-health build-out moving from announcement into
operation, and a maternal mortality data release that the system
itself initiated. The priorities below take those realities as the
starting point rather than work around them.

## 1. Open Cascade Behavioral Health Center Phase 2 on schedule, in public

Phase 1 of the Lents facility opened in September 2025. Phase 2 — 46
additional crisis stabilization beds and an outpatient clinic — is
on the schedule for Q4 2026. We will publish a quarterly construction
and staffing dashboard beginning Q2 2026, including bed-day
utilization, average length of stay, and staff vacancy rates by
role. The project's funders (Multnomah County and the Oregon Health
Authority) have asked for that data; we should publish it whether
or not they ask twice.

## 2. Talk plainly about the nursing contract

Pacific Northwest Health and the Oregon Nurses Association are still
in negotiation. Our public communications should not characterize
the talks as productive when they are difficult, nor should they
characterize ONA's positions as unreasonable. Where management has
moved on a position, we will say so on the record. Where ONA has
moved, we will say that too, with their consent. The 2024 strike
was costly for patients and for staff, and we will not pretend
otherwise.

## 3. Publish maternal mortality data, including the comparisons that look bad

The Maternal Health Initiative's first full-year outcome report
will be released in February 2026. The data show severe maternal
morbidity rates among Black and Indigenous patients that are
substantially higher than the system average. The report does not
hide that. Public communications around the release should foreground
the gap, the system's interventions to date (the doula program, the
fourth-trimester clinic), and the interventions that have not yet
moved the numbers. We will not lead with the system-average
improvement.

## 4. Be specific about the rural capacity gap

Rural Connect Telehealth is what the system can offer Hood River and
Astoria today. It is not the same as inpatient capacity. Our 2026
communications about rural access should describe both the program
and its limits. If a critical-access patient in Astoria needs
inpatient cardiac care, that patient is being transferred to
Portland, and that fact belongs in the public record.

## 5. Maintain editorial firewalls on sponsored coverage

Pacific Northwest Health continues to underwrite The Cascade
Tribune's health desk under a multi-year agreement that excludes
coverage of the system's labor relations, billing practices, and
state regulatory filings. We expect critical reporting in those
areas and will not request changes to it.

## What we will not do

- We will not run a system-wide rebrand campaign in 2026. The Lents
  facility opening and the maternal data release are the year's
  public moments.
- We will not use patient stories in any campaign without
  individually negotiated, written consent — and we will not pay for
  that consent.
- We will not extend underwriting relationships to outlets that
  cannot maintain editorial firewalls on labor and billing coverage.
