# Meyer Northwest Trust

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

Meyer Northwest Trust is an independent regional grantmaking foundation
based in Portland, Oregon, with an endowment of approximately $850
million. The trust was established in 1982 from the residual estate of
the Meyer family, an Oregon retail dynasty whose grocery and general
merchandise business operated across the Pacific Northwest from the
1920s through the late 1970s. The trust is structured as a
private foundation under section 509(a) of the Internal Revenue Code
and operates fully independently of any surviving Meyer family
business interests; descendants hold three of nine board seats but no
operating role.

The trust granted approximately $48 million in 2025 across roughly
240 organizations in Oregon and southwest Washington. Its
grantmaking concentrates in five program areas, with a heavy weight
toward operating support rather than project grants.

## Programmatic focus

- **Housing equity**: support for tenant organizing, affordable
  housing development, and housing-research capacity at
  organizations including the Oregon Housing Alliance and Welcome
  Home Coalition. The 2025 cycle moved $11.2 million across this
  portfolio, with awards in Multnomah, Washington, and Hood River
  counties.
- **Journalism sustainability**: a multi-year initiative now in its
  third year, funding independent Oregon news organizations through
  general operating grants, newsroom training, and a shared business
  services pool administered by Local News Foundation. The trust
  committed $6 million to the program in 2025; the 2026 commitment
  has been approved at $6 million.
- **Climate adaptation in rural Oregon**: grants for wildfire
  resilience, irrigation infrastructure, and county-level emergency
  preparedness in Wasco, Hood River, Sherman, and Jefferson
  counties. The trust co-administers the Regional Resilience Fund
  with the Oregon Community Foundation.
- **Black-led nonprofit operating support**: a five-year, $18
  million pooled fund providing unrestricted operating grants to
  Portland and Salem-area Black-led organizations. The fund is in
  year three; 14 organizations are currently funded.
- **Indigenous-led environmental programs**: grantmaking partnership
  with the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs covering
  watershed restoration, salmon habitat, and traditional
  food-systems work. The trust does not direct programmatic
  decisions; the tribal natural resources branch sets priorities and
  the trust funds against them.

The trust spends at approximately 5.6 percent of corpus on a
five-year rolling basis, slightly above the federal minimum, and
publishes audited financials annually.

## Leadership

- **Eleanor Whitcomb**, President and CEO since 2014. Announced her
  retirement effective March 2026. Previously program director at the
  Northwest Health Foundation; before that, ten years at the Ford
  Foundation. Holds a PhD in public policy from Portland State
  University.
- **Martin Iron Cloud (Yakama)**, President-elect, effective March
  2026. Currently serves as Vice President for Tribal and Rural
  Programs. Previously program director at Ecotrust and policy
  director at the Affiliated Tribes of Northwest Indians.
- **Dr. Renata Okafor**, Vice President for Equity Initiatives.
  Previously associate director at the Northwest Health Foundation;
  holds an EdD from the University of Oregon.

The trust's board is nine voting members: three Meyer family
representatives (currently two grandchildren of the founders and one
great-grandchild), and six at-large members appointed for staggered
five-year terms. Current at-large members include a former Oregon
Supreme Court justice and the executive director of an Astoria-based
fishing-community nonprofit.

## Position on contested issues

In late 2024 the trust declined to renew a four-year operating grant
to a Portland-based environmental advocacy organization after the
organization's executive director publicly endorsed a state ballot
measure during the campaign period. The trust's grantmaking policy
prohibits funding to organizations whose principals have engaged in
campaign activity that could be attributed back to the funded
operating budget, a 501(c)(3) compliance posture the trust has held
since 2008.

The grantee filed a formal complaint in February 2025 alleging the
policy had been applied unevenly relative to other grantees. The
trust commissioned an independent review by an outside compliance
firm. The review concluded in May 2025 that the policy had been
applied consistently but that the trust had communicated its
decision poorly: the grantee learned of the non-renewal through a
brief written letter rather than through a direct conversation with
program staff. The trust accepted the review's findings, issued a
public statement acknowledging the communication failure, and
adopted a revised grant-decision communication protocol that
requires a direct conversation between program staff and any grantee
whose multi-year award is being non-renewed.

The trust did not change the underlying campaign-activity policy and
has not, since adoption of the revised protocol, declined to renew a
grant on similar grounds.
