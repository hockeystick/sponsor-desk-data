# Leadership — Stumptown Roasters Co-op

_This sponsor is a fictional analogue. Names, leadership, programs, and figures are invented for synthetic-data purposes and are not affiliated with any real company or organization._

This document profiles cooperative leadership at Stumptown Roasters Co-op. The cooperative governance model means "decision-makers" includes both the General Manager and the seven-seat Worker Council. Quotes are drawn from public statements, the cooperative's transparency reports, and trade-press interviews.

## Allison Tate, General Manager

General Manager since the cooperative conversion in 2022. Joined Stumptown in 2014 as a barista at the Hawthorne café; promoted to café operations supervisor in 2017, then Café Operations Director in 2018. BA in environmental studies from Lewis & Clark College. Tate reports to the Worker Council on strategic decisions above the bylaws-defined threshold.

In a December 2024 Sprudge interview about the wholesale-partner unionization campaigns, Tate framed the cooperative's position carefully: "We are not the employer of record at those cafés. We have no authority over labor decisions there. We don't weigh in on labor questions at independently owned businesses that purchase Stumptown coffee, regardless of which way the campaign goes."

## Hugo Tomlinson, Roasting Director

Twelve-year tenure with the company; one of the worker-owners who initiated the cooperative conversion. SCA Q-Grader certified. Tomlinson oversees the Foster-Powell roastery, including the public cupping space, and leads the company's roast-profile development. He is one of two members of the Worker Council elected from the production team.

In an October 2025 Cooperative Grocer Network panel, Tomlinson talked about the cooperative model's roast-quality implications: "When the people on the production line are also voting members of the cooperative, you don't get pressure to cut corners on roast time to hit a quarterly margin number. The incentives are aligned."

## Ines Castellanos, Sourcing Director

Joined Stumptown in 2019 from Counter Culture Coffee in Durham, North Carolina. BA in international agricultural development from UC Davis. Castellanos manages the company's direct-trade relationships with 41 farmer cooperatives and individual producers across six origin countries.

In the cooperative's 2025 Green Coffee Transparency Report, Castellanos wrote: "Our average green coffee purchase price is 28% above the C-market reference. We do not claim that this premium has solved the structural inequalities of the coffee supply chain. The premium is a partial answer. The structural answer is not in our hands."

## Reggie Park, Worker Council Chair

Elected to the Worker Council in 2024, named Chair in 2025. Joined Stumptown in 2018 as a barista at the Foster-Powell café. The Worker Council Chair holds operational sign-off authority for cooperative decisions above the bylaws-defined threshold (currently any single commitment over $250,000, any decision affecting more than 20% of worker-owners, and any change to the cooperative's compensation structure).

## Yumiko Shimazaki, Café Operations Director

Joined Stumptown in 2016 as a barista; promoted to Café Operations Director in 2022. Shimazaki oversees the four Portland cafés and the airport kiosk operation, including hiring, scheduling, and the cooperative's barista training program.

## Decision-makers for sponsorship decisions (2026)

Stumptown's cooperative governance affects the sponsorship decision process. For partnerships under $10,000, the General Manager (Tate) has standing approval. Between $10,000 and $50,000 require approval by a marketing subcommittee that includes Yumiko Shimazaki, the cooperative's Communications Lead (currently a rotating role), and one Worker Council member. Six-figure partnerships require full Worker Council approval — which means the partnership has to make sense to a cross-section of the cooperative's worker-owners, not just the marketing function. This is slower than typical corporate sponsorship decisions; sponsors who need fast turnarounds should not target Stumptown.

Stumptown is most receptive to partnerships that align with the cooperative's two operational priorities: direct-trade origin storytelling and PNW food-and-drink culture. Partnerships outside those priorities rarely clear the Worker Council vote.
